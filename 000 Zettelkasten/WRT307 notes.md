# Chapter 4: Conducting Reader-Centered Research

## Core Idea

Workplace research is **reader-centered** — find what your reader needs to decide or act, not everything you can find on a topic.

---

## Six Qualities of Good Workplace Research

1. **Focused** — targets reader's goals; cut irrelevant material
2. **Thorough** — complete enough for the reader to act confidently
3. **Credible** — holds up to scrutiny in the reader's eyes
4. **Evidence-Based** — results → analysis → conclusions → recommendations, each grounded in evidence
5. **Unbiased** — unbiased sources, unbiased presentation
6. **Ethical** — methods and recommendations align with reader's ethical standards

---

## Gathering Information

- Define research goals from your reader's questions and needs
- Consider what _type_ of answers they want (general vs. specific, quantitative vs. testimonial)
- Include relevant info the reader didn't ask for if it affects their decision
- **Primary research** = you generate it (experiments, surveys, observations)
- **Secondary research** = reading others' work (journals, articles, encyclopedias)
- Start broad (secondary overviews), then go specific
- Four source types for people-related topics: persons affected, persons involved, similar organizations, professional publications
- Evaluate sources: relevant? credible? accurate? complete? current? unbiased?
- Watch for your own biases when evaluating sources
- Take careful notes: distinguish quotes vs. paraphrases, source ideas vs. your own, and record full bibliographic info

---

## Analyzing Information

- Revisit your research goals before analyzing — don't get sidetracked
- Arrange data visually (tables, charts, graphs) to _see_ patterns
- Look for meaningful relationships: compare outcomes, compare process steps, compare sources, consider cause/effect and correlations
- **Analyze subgroups** — enables targeted, refined decisions rather than broad ones

---

## Critical Thinking

- **Anchoring** — tendency to stick with your first intuition and only find confirming evidence. Actively seek alternatives.
- **Value counterevidence** — contradictions signal the need for deeper investigation or hedged conclusions
- **Check personal/organizational bias** — view data from your reader's and other stakeholders' perspectives

---

## Drawing Conclusions

- Choose the interpretation most **actionable** for your reader (e.g., "the department can improve outreach" > "students are irresponsible")
- **Acknowledge uncertainty** — use "suggests" / "appears to indicate" instead of "proves"; present alternative interpretations when appropriate
- **Explain significance** — connect your conclusion to the specific decision or action it supports

---

## Making Recommendations

- Be **specific and actionable**, not just a restatement of conclusions
- Back recommendations with research showing the suggested action is likely to work
- Weak: "improve advising" → Strong: "establish training sessions and have high-performing departments share strategies"

---

## Intellectual Property & Documentation

- **Copyright** applies the moment a work is created (no © needed)
- **Fair use** depends on: proportion used, publication status, economic impact
- **Fair use does NOT apply to individual graphics** — always need permission
- **Web content is copyrighted** — being online ≠ public domain
- Public domain: government works, expired copyrights, explicitly released works
- Employer-created work can generally be reused internally

### When to Cite at Work

- Got permission? → cite it
- Common knowledge _in your field_? → no citation needed (broader than school standard)
- Employer owns it? → generally no citation needed
- Taking credit for someone else's work? → never acceptable
- Writing for a journal? → cite everything rigorously
- When in doubt → ask your boss or instructor
